White Space and Clean Design

Yale School of Art

getquip.com

I chose the website for Quip because it is a beautiful exampled of white space and clean design. I love that it is straight to the point, there is no question about what the site is for and that it is all spaced well. The designer clearly had a good grasp of white space because the entire site utilizes this principle -- as you scroll down the page, nothing is too cluttered or busy; there are some designs that can be a bit TOO simple, in this case, however, the minimalist approach is countered with motion graphics rather than still images to create a beautiful and visually interesting compostiion.

Proximity and Grouping

Kansas City, Missouri

kmco.gov

This is a site that I have actually been referencing quite a bit when it comes to desinging a webpage. For another course I'm currently studying, we were tasked with redesigning the Loup County webpage (http://www.co.loup.ne.us/). Kansas City, Missouri's site does a great job of utilizing proximity to help the view understand which information is at their disposal and what it relates to. Certain elements are close together than others to indicate that it is a group or related content but not so close that it gets crammed and confusing. For example, certain images that correlate with specific text are closer to each other; meanwhile, you have buttons that are in close proximity to text to indicate they are linked to the same information.

Alignment

Ling's Cars

lingscars.com

Alright... where do I even start with this one? The principle I chose to tie it with is alignment, but as I'm sure you can tell there are a LOT of issues going on here. In regards to alignment, right off the bat we can see there is no grid being followed. The padding/margins to the left are significantly larger and more empty than the right because the designer chose to add extra information ONLY on the right side. There is WAY too much going on that it's hard to even tell that it's a car rental website. Far too much contrast and the visual hierarchy is all over the place -- not sure where to even look. For me, this is a fantastic example of exactly what NOT to do.
